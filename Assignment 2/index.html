2020 Global Top 2000 Companies

Forbes has published its Global 2000 ranking, which showcases the worlds 2,000 public companies as of May 13 2020. These corporate giants collectively represent a $42.3 trillion, in revenues. Contribute $3.3 trillion in profits. Furthermore they command assets $201.4 trillion. Possess a combined market capitalization of $54.3 trillion. It is important to note that although sales and assets have experienced growth compared to the year there has been a decline in profits and market capitalization.

The unexpected turbulence in markets due to the COVID 19 pandemic has significantly impacted the market values of companies listed in the Global 2000 ranking. The market values presented in this ranking were determined as of April 30th reflecting the shifts within the landscape. This years threshold for entry into this group of companies was set at a market value of $5.27 billion, which is notably lower than the preceding years threshold of $6.25 billion.

This interactive dashboard provides an exploration of the Global 2000 companies offering a view of their geographic presence, regional distribution and financial performance. By utilizing this tool you can gain insights, into the corporate landscape.

LOCATION

The map visualization gives an overview of the distribution of the Top 2000 companies locations. Each marker represents a country and the markers, on the map are color coded to show their continents. The size of each marker reflects the mean market value, which is calculated by dividing the market value by the number of Top 2000 companies, in that particular location.

Notably, among all the countries, The Netherlands distinguishes itself with a market value of $32.23 billion. This observation comes as no surprise, as The Netherlands boasts an above-average total market value while having a smaller number of Top 2000 companies compared to other nations. This unique combination is a contributing factor to its elevated market value.

Conversely, Greece holds the distinction of having the lowest mean market value among all countries, amounting to a mere $2.24 billion. While it does maintain a presence in the double digits with ten Top 2000 companies, its relatively low total market value places it at the bottom of the list.

In contrast countries, like China, the United Kingdom, Japan and the United States have total market values but also more Top 2000 companies. This slightly reduces their market values compared to The Netherlands. However these countries still have a higher average market values compared to other nations.

Lastly, while there are only two countries in Oceania that house Top 2000 companies, Australia's ability to achieve a notably high mean market value is indeed an impressive accomplishment, which emphasizing its significant economic position, on a global scale.

So after examining their geographical locations and assessing their mean market values, what does the distribution of the number of Top 2000 companies look like across continents and countries?

DISTRIBUTION

The left radial chart illustrates the percentage distribution of the Top 2000 companies across continents, while the right radial chart displays the distribution of Top 2000 companies by country. Both charts are color-coded to represent the respective continents.

Looking at the continent-wise distribution, Asia's dominance in housing the highest number of Top 2000 companies is a reflection of its economic prowess. Notably, China and Japan, both Asian powerhouses, stand out as major contributors to this dominance. With their thriving business landscapes, they consistently make a significant impact on the global corporate stage. The impressive number of companies from these countries further solidifies their positions as key players in the international business arena.

However, it's important to emphasize that while Asia leads the way, North America and Europe should not be underestimated. These continents have robust economies and diverse business landscapes, resulting in a substantial representation of Top 2000 companies. The United States, in particular, takes a commanding lead in terms of the sheer number of enterprises, with 588 companies making the list. This underscores the country's economic might and its position as a global economic leader.

China, following closely behind the U.S. with 367 companies, is not just a regional force but also a significant contender on the world stage. The same can be said for Japan, with 217 companies, showcasing its enduring corporate strength and influence.

In Europe, the United Kingdom's 77 companies and Canada's 61 companies are notable contributors to the regional and global business landscape. These figures underscore their respective roles as key players in Europe and North America, contributing to the overall diversity and strength of the Top 2000 ranking.

In summary, while Asia's dominance is evident, a deeper analysis reveals that the Top 2000 companies are spread across continents, highlighting the global nature of these enterprises and the multifaceted contributions of various regions to the corporate world.

Now that we've concluded our examination of regional data, let's delve deeper into understanding the financial performance of these companies.

RATE

Brush the chart for a clearer view.

The first bubble chart offers a detailed examination of the interplay between a company's Assets and its Sales, providing insights into their connection. In this visualization, the size of each bubble serves as a visual representation of the Assets Turnover Rate. This rate is meticulously calculated by dividing a company's Sales by its Total Assets, shedding light on how effectively the company leverages its assets to generate revenue.

Similarly, the second bubble chart delves into the relationship between a company's Assets and its Profits. Each bubble's size signifies the Return On Assets Ratio, a crucial metric derived by dividing Total Profits by Total Assets. This ratio provides a deeper understanding of a company's profitability concerning its asset portfolio. It unveils how efficiently the company utilizes its assets to generate profits, showcasing its effectiveness in translating asset strength into financial success.

Both of these charts are thoughtfully color-coded by continent.

In our comprehensive analysis, we discern a distinct positive correlation between a company's Assets and its Sales/Profits. Companies positioned in the right quadrant of the chart, such as ICBC and China Construction Bank, exemplify substantial Asset bases and commendable Sales/Profits performance. Take ICBC, for instance, a banking giant that adeptly harnesses its assets to yield substantial revenue, exemplifying its operational prowess.

Notably, among this select group, Exxon Mobil emerges as a standout performer. Leveraging its substantial asset portfolio, Exxon Mobil achieves an Assets Turnover Ratio surpassing 1 and a robust Return On Assets Ratio exceeding 0.05. This impressive performance underscores Exxon Mobil's exceptional ability to efficiently convert its assets into substantial sales and profits, marking it as an exemplar in the realm of asset utilization and profitability.

At last, let's focus specifically on the financial performance of the top 20 global companies.

RANKING

The grouped bar chart provides a comprehensive view of the world's top 20 companies, organized by their global rankings. This chart offers valuable insights into these companies, showcasing key financial metrics such as Sales, Profits, Assets, and Market Value.

Among the distinguished occupants of this elite group is this year's top-ranked company, ICBC (Industrial and Commercial Bank of China). ICBC's prominent presence is notable, as it represents one of the three colossal state-owned Chinese banks. Additionally, joining the ranks are financial behemoth JPMorgan Chase and technology giants such as Apple and Samsung Electronics. These companies, renowned for their global influence, command a significant position in the chart's top 20.

Upon closer examination of the chart, a discerning observation emerges: there is no apparent uniform trend among the top 20 companies in terms of their Sales, Profits, Assets, and Market Value. This lack of a discernible pattern is entirely normal, as their rankings are determined by a comprehensive evaluation encompassing all these factors. Each company's unique strengths and market dynamics contribute to its individual ranking and financial performance.

However, it's worth noting that the top 3 companies—ICBC, China Construction Bank, and JPMorgan Chase—stand out with notably high Asset values compared to their peers within this elite group. One plausible explanation for this pattern could be their status as state enterprises. State-owned companies often possess extensive assets, given their pivotal role in their respective national economies. This leads to a higher concentration of assets among these top-ranking entities.

In summary, this chart offers a window into the financial performance of the world's top 20 companies, showcasing a diverse array of businesses and industries. While no uniform trends emerge among these elite performers, the top 3 companies, notably ICBC, China Construction Bank, and JPMorgan Chase, exhibit a distinctive emphasis on assets, potentially influenced by their state-owned status and significant economic roles.
